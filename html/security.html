Steps I take to protect my security and privacy

1. Introduction

Our security and privacy is constantly being threatened, either because new, ever more sophisticated exploits are being discovered, or because some entities (governments, ISPs, companies, etc) are pushing against the privacy of their own citizens/users.

One doesn't need to look far to see the stagering amount of vulnerabilities recently uncovered, and how these might (have) affect(ed) you:

  • Spectre & Meltdown
  • Log4Shell
  • Heartbleed
  • LogoFail

As if the above wasn't bad enough, over the years numerous attempts have been made to weaken the privacy of online denizens, as well as other scandals about data misuse, including, but not limited to:

  • PRISM
  • Cambridge Analytica

In this post I'll talk briefly about what measures I've taken in order to secure my systems and protect my privacy, what they protect against and how I went about implementing them.

2. Security vs Privacy

Contrary to what some of you may think, security and privacy don't exactly mean the same thing.

While privacy requires security, security just by itself doesn't guarantee privacy.

2.1. Let me try to exemplify

Imagine you live in a house with no door, nor any other measures that prevent unauthorized personnel from invading your home.

In this case, its obvious that the house isn't secure, and neither is it private, since if people can get in and out, nothing is stopping them from snooping on your activities, on any documents and letters you might have lying around.

2.2. Now lets look at another example

Now you have a house with a strong (locked) door, sturdy walls.

… But your house is entirely made of glass 1…

Super strong, totally bulletproof glass… But glass nonetheless!

Now your house is pretty secure, no one can get in without you opening the door for them. Yet, it isn't really private, is it? Everyone can just see what you're doing, at all time.

3. Privacy measures

3.1. Avoid non-privacy respecting services

While this one might be the most obvious, this is actually pretty hard to do.

I don't have any social media accounts, more surprisingly, I don't have a Google account!

But I still use Google services, mostly Search and Youtube, since they are better than competing offers, or, in the case of Youtube, since its where all the content creators are.

And I use Whatsapp exclusively as my messaging app on Android, despite its parent company being criticized over the years for their stance towards privacy.

Some of these services are really hard to replace due to either their enormous quality gap compared to alternatives (in the case of Google Search) or because of Network Effect (in the case of Youtube and Whatsapp).

Despite that I have duckduckgo as my default search engine, and get a lot of mileage out of the Bangs.

As for Youtube, I either download the video with yt-dlp and view them in mpv 2, or use Invidious.

For my email I use Proton Mail.

The choice to opt for more privacy respecting services helps limit the bulk of data collected about me.

3.2. Minimize digital footprint

I frequently trim unused accounts, not only deleting my credentials but also asking for websites to delete all information they have stored on me.

This is surprisingly easy with GDPR, even if the website doesn't provide a straightforward way to delete accounts, chances are the privacy policy includes a helpful link just for that.

This is important as it limits the consequences and risk of data leakage should someone breach your account or compromise the service provider.

3.3. Tweak the browser

One of the first things I do when I switch browsers (something I do surprisingly often), is look through the settings page to try to improve privacy and security, this includes disabling 3rd party cookies, enabling their respective "reduced tracking" modes, enabling any anti-popups, tweaking the default website permissions, disabling those annoying notification prompts, etc…

I also am one of the few that browses the web with javascript off by default, only enabling it on a domain by domain basis.

Since my web browsing is quite basic (mostly looking at forum/blog posts and reading documentation) this doesn't affect me much 3, and improves my security substancially, considering I'm not running untrusted Javascript code everytime I open a web page.

3.4. Aliases and Pseudonyms

I usually avoid using my personal email to sign up for accounts, this is mostly related to spam issues than to privacy per se, still, email aliases have the advantage of segmenting different accounts more cleanly.

When I require help or wish to comment on some post/respond to another comment, I always use a pseudonym (like most people I suppose, outside social media like Facebook and Instagram), usually one per website.

4.1. Password managers

Most of my passwords reside on my password manager, with the exception of device credentials and the password managers password (for obvious reasons).

All passwords are randomly generated and are at least 16 characters long, including lower and upper case characters, symbols and numerals.

I only know a couple of credentials from the top of my head:

  1. My computer login
  2. My password managers password
  3. My phone pin
  4. My (work) email

That's it!

I can't even remember my debit card details! Although this may possibly be related to the fact I don't have a lot of income…

Although it might seem scary to store most of your credentials in the same place, the advantages more than make up for it, it eliminates the problem of having to recall your credentials (with the exception of the master password) and is probably safer than other alternatives, like storing passwords on paper, or in an unencrypted text file.

4.2. Multi-factor Authentication

I always activate 2FA on services that have the option (roughly 50% of them), the rest either use SMS 2FA, which isn't as secure, or they don't have any kind of 2 factor.

I even considered buying a Physical Security Key recently, but I decided against it, since support for them is even more rare than other options.

2FA ensures that, should an attacker obtain your credentials, they still would need to have access to yet another secret to obtain compromise your account.

The 2 most popular 2FA methods are SMS and TOTP tokens.

Many non-computer savvy users already use multi-factor authentication without even being aware of it, when using an ATM (credit/debit card + PIN).

4.3. Signatures

I've recently taken to signing both my emails and git commits.

Basically Digital Signatures are a way to ensure message Authenticity, or to put it another way, they guarantee that a given message came from us and hasn't been tampered with (be it in transit or somewhere else).

My email service of choice makes signing emails as easy as toggling the option in the settings menu, as for other email providers, Thunderbird provides a relatively easy way to signing emails.

To automatically sign git commits, you must set the commit.gpgsign variable to true, and set user.signingkey to your key ID.

If you use Github as your Forge, you can add your key using the web interface, from then on, Github will display a Verified badge next to all your signed commits.

4.4. Adblocking

Apart from also being a good privacy practise (due to reducing tracking by 3rd parties), adblocking is also largely a security measure, due to the rampant scams, fake download buttons, popups that redirect you to malicious sites…

The removal of advertisements and tracking also substancially improves browsing speeds, since it reduces the amount of data the browser must download when first loading a page.
